Design Proposal · v3 — Grounded in the real storefront · Internal/technical reference

Not "a" flower. Your flower.

Third pass, after two rounds of feedback and three new photos of the actual shop. Everything invented in v1/v2 — the script logo, the ivory-and-gold palette, the borrowed French-patisserie mood — is retired. This version is built from what's already on your storefront sign, your pink boxes, and your own Instagram graphic: a geometric flower mark, a bold wordmark, bubblegum pink, and a black ribbon accent — now paired with a warm coffee-brown sampled from a Flora Pastel promo graphic instead of the sign's navy. It also breaks the grid on purpose, says less, and uses your real product photography instead of gradients wherever the resolution allows.

Round 3 — five specific corrections

Grounded, imperfect, unmistakably Flora.

1 · Too perfectGrid intentionally broken: hero copy sits off-center, the story photo bleeds past the container and the headline overlaps it, the catalogue grid staggers instead of aligning.
2 · Too much textStorytelling copy cut to one line + one short sentence, max. A "copy law" is now a written rule in section 02, not just a v3 fix.
3 · No real identityThe invented script logo and gold accents are gone. In: your actual flower mark, brand pink, a coffee-brown sampled from your own promo graphics, and a mosaic gallery device lifted straight from your own Instagram post.
4 · Hero, more ambitiousFull-bleed photo, transparent nav, and nothing else: mark, wordmark, one tagline. No button, no card, no subcopy paragraph.
5 · Photography is the bottleneckPlaceholders replaced with real crops from your product photo and storefront wherever resolution allows — see the 70/30 callout in section 02.
01

Design analysis

Three new photos changed the brief: the storefront, the interior, and an existing promotional graphic. These aren't mood-board inspiration — they're the actual brand. Everything below is sampled or redrawn directly from them.

What v1/v2 got wrong

Invented (v1 → v2)

Cursive script logo, ivory/gold/dusty-rose palette, dark-chocolate ink, a generic "French patisserie" mood borrowed from Ladurée and Cédric Grolet — none of it traceable to Flora Pastel specifically.

Real (v3)

A geometric flower mark already on the sign. Bubblegum pink from the awning and packaging. A warm coffee-brown from their own "Martes de Panqués" promo graphic, replacing the sign's navy for a softer, more edible tone. A black ribbon from the actual boxes. A diagonal photo-mosaic device from their own Instagram post.

Grid & symmetry

  • v2 was correct in every measurement and boring for it — nothing was off-balance.
  • v3 hero copy sits left-of-center, low in the frame, never dead-center.
  • Story section: photo bleeds past the container edge; headline card overlaps the photo instead of sitting beside it.
  • Catalogue grid: one plate staggers down from its neighbors — a deliberate, not accidental, misalignment.

Copy discipline

  • New rule, not a one-off: every storytelling section gets one short line, max one sentence.
  • Hero: mark + wordmark + a single tagline. Nothing explains itself.
  • Catalogue captions drop the description line entirely — name, price, "Preguntar" is enough.

Ownable elements

  • The flower mark, redrawn as a reusable SVG — usable as a favicon, a watermark, a photo-mask.
  • The diagonal mosaic gallery, adapted from their own Instagram post into a real responsive component.
  • A thin black ribbon-line, echoing the box packaging, as a section divider.
  • None of these three exist anywhere else — they can't be swapped onto another bakery's site.

Hero, taken further

  • Nav goes fully transparent over the photo — no bar, no background, just a gradient scrim at the very top for legibility.
  • No button, no CTA row, no subcopy paragraph — the ask example ("El sabor de los momentos importantes.") is used verbatim.
  • Nav becomes solid only after the visitor scrolls past the hero.

Photography reality check

  • Existing product photography (the nut-crusted cake, the berry cake, the cheesecakes) is genuinely good — cropped, it holds up at catalogue size.
  • It does not hold up at full-bleed hero size — resolution runs out. The hero placeholder stays a labeled placeholder, honestly.
  • Section 02 sets a concrete shot list so a proper hero shoot is a scoped, one-time cost — not a vague ongoing risk.

What stays from v2

  • Rectangular, restrained buttons over v1's pill-everywhere — still correct, kept.
  • Photography-led catalogue over ecommerce cards — still correct, kept, now with real photos.
  • Fraunces for editorial section headlines — kept, now paired with the real flower mark and coffee-brown wordmark instead of a script logo.

Copy law: every storytelling section gets one headline + one sentence, maximum. If it needs a second sentence, it's two sections, not one long one.

The flower mark is a placeholder, not a redraw to ship.

It was recreated by eye from a photo of the sign for this proposal only — close enough to communicate the idea, not accurate enough to publish. Do not use this SVG in production. Reserve the space in every component (favicon, nav, watermark, mosaic diamond) and drop in the client's official vector logo the moment it's provided; nothing else in the system needs to change when that happens.

Minimum shot list for the hero to work: 1–2 single-subject "hero" pieces, shot wide (not square), high resolution (2400px+), consistent light direction, on a neutral surface, styled sparse (no busy backdrop) — everything the current square social-media crops weren't shot for. Everything else in this system can ship with what you already have.

A hero that survives "good," not just "extraordinary" — without disguising the food

First attempt at this section used a color-wash and a large watermark over the photo — wrong call, reverted. In food, real color sells: mute the gold crust and the cream texture under a magenta gradient and the photo stops reading as something to eat. The actual fix isn't a filter, it's size and isolation — the same untouched catalogue photo, given room to be the only thing on the screen, already reads as a hero. No color shift, no logo on top of the cake:

This doesn't remove the need for a proper hero shoot — a dedicated shot will always look better — it just means launch isn't blocked on getting one perfect photo first. The design's job is to get out of the photo's way, not to compensate for it.

05

Mobile adaptations

The offset/overlap layouts from the desktop story section had to survive a 320px frame without becoming illegible — that's the real test of "breaking the grid on purpose" rather than by accident.

Hero content stays low + leftNever re-centers on mobile — the asymmetry is a deliberate choice, not a desktop-only flourish that collapses to "safe" centered text on small screens.
Story overlap, simplified not removedThe image-bleed + overlapping text-card pattern survives on mobile as a photo with a card overlapping its bottom-left corner — same idea, fewer pixels.
Catalogue staggerThe second plate in the grid drops down slightly even on a 2-column mobile layout — the asymmetry is a system rule, not a desktop-only effect.
WhatsApp FABFixed bottom-right, appears once the visitor scrolls past the transparent hero (so it never fights the deliberately empty hero for attention).

Desktop layout

At ≥1024px the hero gets a full-height, chrome-free moment, and the story section's photo-bleed + overlapping text card becomes the clearest single proof that this system doesn't default to a perfectly balanced grid.

Story sectionPhoto is 56% width pinned right and bleeds to the container edge; the copy card overlaps its top-left corner, breaking the grid on purpose.
